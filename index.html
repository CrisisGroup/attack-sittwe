Ten Conflicts to Watch in 2026

The world was careening into a dangerous new era well before Donald Trump’s return to the White House. But the risks are getting higher rather than lower in his second term thus far.

by Comfort Ero and Richard Atwood

Published 31 December 2025

The past year has seen fighting rage on in many wars around the globe. U.S. President Donald Trump has put himself centre stage in many trouble spots. He has brought fresh energy to peacemaking, after years in which diplomatic efforts to end conflicts had been flailing.

But Trump has not calmed global turmoil – in many cases, he has made it worse. His deals have brought respite to some battlefields but no lasting peace. Meanwhile, his lawlessness and spheres-of-influence risk giving licence to other states tempted by adventurism.

The year ahead seems likely to be as bloody as the one drawing to a close. Here are the International Crisis Group’s Ten Conflicts to Watch in 2026.

VENEZUELA

The U.S. has commenced its biggest military buildup in the Caribbean Sea in decades, seemingly set on removing Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro from power. In December, the coast guard began seizing tankers exporting Venezuelan crude in what Trump called a blockade.

Whether the two sides can find an off-ramp remains unclear. Ousting Maduro militarily is more likely to go wrong than not, potentially triggering chaos, refugee flight and a protracted low-intensity conflict.

SUDAN

The war in Sudan grinds on toward de facto partition, with the army and paramilitary Rapid Support Forces each controlling swathes of territory. As the human toll mounts, outside powers are still doing more to fuel the war than bring it to an end.

Washington should do more to press the warring parties’ outside sponsors to back a permanent ceasefire, along with steps to establish a civilian-led transitional government. It will be a tall order to piece Sudan back together, but a truce must come first.

Ethiopia-Eritrea

Meanwhile, two of Sudan’s neighbours, Ethiopia and Eritrea, are trading barbs. Addis Ababa accuses Eritrea of meddling in Ethiopia’s internal conflicts; Asmara suspects landlocked Ethiopia of coveting an Eritrean seaport.

A distracted world is paying insufficient attention to growing tensions. The last Ethiopia-Eritrea war killed tens of thousands. Another could tip the Horn of Africa into all-out conflagration.

Mali and Burkina Faso

In September, militants linked to al-Qaeda imposed a partial blockade on the Malian capital Bamako, highlighting the deepening crisis in the central Sahel. In both Mali and neighbouring Burkina Faso, military regimes have failed to beat back jihadist groups.

Absent drastic change, things look bleak. Authorities in both landlocked countries should consider mending ties with coastal West African states to safeguard routes to seaports. They need to contemplate dialogue with insurgents, too. It’s an uncertain course, but few other options remain.

Ukraine

Russian forces continue to grind ahead, though at tremendous cost and without breaking Ukrainian lines. Ukraine shows no sign of giving up, despite its own significant losses. What’s changed over the past year is less the battlefield than the politics – thanks to Trump’s upending of U.S. support for Ukraine and its president.

How events will unfold is hard to know, particularly if Trump pushes harder to impose a deal skewed toward Moscow on Kyiv and its European backers.

SYRIA

Since seizing power in late 2024, Ahmed al-Sharaa has won over a sceptical world. But the new Syrian president’s gains abroad could be undone if he cannot curb sectarian bloodletting at home.

Other challenges are also formidable. Even with sanctions lifted, jump-starting the war-ravaged economy will be difficult. Syrians, especially the Sunni Arab majority, are still hopeful but frustrated with al-Sharaa’s concentration of power. The north east remains outside central control, and the spectre of further Israeli intervention or Turkish military action against Kurdish militants is ever present.

Israel-Palestine

The bloodiest war in a century of Arab-Israeli conflict ground to a tenuous halt in October. But Gaza remains in limbo, its surviving population crowded in terrible conditions into less than half the strip, much of it reduced to rubble and under continued Israeli fire. In the West Bank, the hardest-right government in Israeli history seems bent on annexation.

The immediate question is whether diplomats can push the Gaza ceasefire past a first phase of hostage-prisoner exchanges. The bodies envisioned for the enclave’s governance have yet to be established. Security provisions are murkier still.

Israel and the U.S. versus Iran and the Houthis

The lull following June’s twelve-day war gave all sides a measure of satisfaction: Israel and the U.S. did great damage to the Iranian nuclear and missile programs, while Iran’s regime remained standing after absorbing the blows.

But Israel may well strike again, if Iran moves to rebuild either program. Hostilities next time around are likely to be worse. The U.S. and Iran should shore up the shaky truce.

MYANMAR

The military regime that seized power in 2021, driving Myanmar into civil war, has gained the upper hand after a period in which rebels looked ascendant. China has thrown its weight behind the junta, while other Asian powers assume that military control will persist after a sham vote installs a nominally civilian government.

The regime’s opponents have no evident strategy for dislodging it but the war is far from over. All these developments bode ill for the Rohingya, whether those in Bangladesh or those remaining in Myanmar.

AFGHANISTAN-PAKISTAN

Afghanistan and Pakistan came to blows in early October, after Islamabad attributed a surge in attacks on Pakistani territory to insurgents based across the Afghan border. A ceasefire is holding for now, but bilateral tensions have not eased.

Diplomatic ties are suspended. Trade has ceased. Pakistan is deporting masses of Afghan nationals. Another round of fighting is likely should militant violence persist.
